They might be challenging to develop, but they are essential for any company and the foundation of a strong corporate or even personal brand. A good logo should:

  • Be distinctive.

  • Be enduring

  • Be memorable

  • Work good in all sizes

  • Include the vibe of your brand.


What on earth is a brand vibe, and how do I locate mine?


What you want your brand to evoke in consumers is a certain atmosphere. It may also occasionally be referred to as a brand personality. It should be specified in your brand guidelines and be consistent with your logo and other material. We want our law companies to feel reliable and reputable, just like we want our yoga instructors to feel serene and kind, and we want our party planners to feel well-prepared and entertaining.

With me so far? The process of creating a logo becomes 100 times simpler once you understand your brand vibe* (*not supported by science). As you read the recommendations for designing a logo, keep your brand mood in mind. Also, take some time to consider how each logo makes you feel. Top Logo Design Tips & Tricks


Are you prepared to begin? Below are our top ten logo design hints and tips:


1. A picture is worth 1,000 words


Since a logo serves as a visual representation of your brand, there is no need to describe what you do; simply demonstrate it. Use straightforward icons to convey your identity.


2. To keep your logo design tidy, use empty space


Keep your logo "clean" (designer lingo for "plenty of vacant space") to ensure that people can read it at a distance or when it's incredibly small. Here, we can see how Savant Yoga made use of white space to elicit a sense of serenity. Utilizing white space in your logo design is also beneficial when it comes to printing marketing materials like t-shirts, posters, brochures, and posters. It is simpler to smoothly include your logo into many styles and formats.


3. Use shapes


The use of shapes may truly help your logo stand out. For instance, to create a professional-looking logo for a law office, we could place the name of the firm inside boxes. This aids with cross-platform branding because a "boxed in" logo looks good on letterhead, in presentations, on products like pens or lanyards, as well as digitally. Shapes with eye-catching textures or gradients can elevate your design to a new level.


4. Imagine your logo in situ


When creating your logo, try to keep in mind where it will be used. Will it be on a uniform or will it only be on your website design? To preview how your logo will seem in context, you can utilize a mock-up generator. When designing a logo, it's a good idea to consider your marketing strategy. Make a logo that looks nice on business cards if you plan to network extensively.


5. Colour is key for good logo design


Not all monochromatic artwork is black and white. Black and white can occasionally appear harsh to the eyes, especially when trying to convey a zen-like mood. To generate subtle contrasts in your logo, utilize several tones of the same colour.

Make that thing your logo if your name is something tangible. There's a reason Apple's logo is an apple; don't be scared to push into the obvious. Be aware that some companies have to take legal action to prevent competitors from using their trademarks infringingly because the wording in their logos were either not translated into the local tongue or the logos themselves portrayed something that wasn't translated.

For instance, a South African winery named Jordan Winery sued Lovane for exploiting chameleon imagery on their products. Jordan Winery was utilizing the chameleon image on their labels. The issue was that the word "uluvane" in one of the national languages meant "chameleon," which would lead to links between the two brands. If Jordan Winery had employed trademark translation to safeguard its name, the two brands might not have had to spend so much time in court and money on legal bills.


7. Be authoritative with your logo


Be precise, but make sure it works for your company. Non-profit organizations, for example, require a level of seriousness that a production firm or ice cream shop can avoid. Ask yourself if you would trust this business if you saw its logo for the first time. Here, Helping Hands has created a logo that incorporates hands while yet being respectable. Serif fonts and subdued colours are crucial in preventing the logo from feeling overly comical.


8. A splash of color can increase visual interest


This is referred to as "visual salience" in design. It's a terrific method to develop a memorable logo as well as a great term to use in casual conversation to impress your designer buddies. This little splash of colour prevents your design from looking flat and is a tactic designers frequently employ. The Amazon logo is an excellent illustration of this.

As a brand, Pepsi has been around for more than 120 years. The Pepsi logo that we are familiar with today is very different from how it originally appeared.
